Part 1: DAOST Chapter 2 Questions

  • Explain in your own words the point of the jitter plot.

    • The point of a jitter plot is to get a better idea of the frequency of data points in a dot plot. E.g. you have a dot plot and many data points have the same value, therefore they are drawn onto the same point. When making a small jitter to each data point you can better see how the data is distributed.

  • Explain in your own words the point of figure 2-3.

    • In figure 2.3. the author is explaining the importance of choosing the right bin alignment and pointing out that for the same data it clearly matters how you choose the bin alignment as it changes the visualisation and interpretation of the data (you can tweek the bin alignment for a half a bin width in both directions as is shown in image 2-3). Because of this, histograms can be sometimes indefinite. Also they can sometimes be a poor method to determine distribution’s shape as they are really affected by the number of used bins. (Image 2.3. from Data Analysis with Open Source Tools by Philipp K. Janert).

  • The author of DAOST (Philipp Janert) likes KDEs (and think they're better than histograms). And I don't. When can KDEs be misleading?

    • When you want to know exactly the amount of certain points(or points within a bin) you can’t see what the right amount is. It's less clear to get a specific information from the KDE plots then from histograms.

  • I've discussed some strengths of the CDF - there are also weaknesses. Janert writes "[CDFs] have less intuitive appeal than histograms or KDEs". What does he mean by that?

    • What he means is that people find histograms or KDEs more understandable than CFDs. This is because the eye is not as good at judging area versus distances, therefore it is easier to read of a KDE or histogram as we can see the peaks in the data or tell us how probable it is to find a data point with a certain value. People have a harder time understanding how the CDF is working as it represents an area.

  • What is a Quantile plot? What is it good for.

    • Quantile plot is a plot where the axes of a CDF plot have been switched, so the percentage is now on the x-axis. The information contained in a Quantile plot is the same as in a CDF graph. Quantile plots can be useful when needing to know which data corresponds to a certain percentile of the dataset.

  • How is a Probability plot defined? What is it useful for? Have you ever seen one before?

    • A probability plot is used to determine whether a set of points behave according to the Gaussian distribution. From the plot it is also possible to read directly off the graph the estimates for the standard deviation and the mean. We have seen these graphs before in physics class from bachelor studies.

  • One of the reasons I like DAOST is that Janert is so suspicious of mean, median and related summary statistics. Explain why one has to be careful when using those - and why visualization of the full data is always better.

    • Because mean, median and related summary statistics do only apply to a data that is unimodal distribution, that is, distributions with a single, central peak (although, the median can be used when the data has not a peak in the middle). Therefore, it is much better to plot the data because then you can see how the data behaves and get better knowledge of the data, as the mean and other summary statistic do not indicate much when the data is not normally distributed.

  • I love box plots. When are box plots most useful?

    • A box plot allows people to see whether the distribution of data is symmetric or not and how the weight is distributed between the tails and the central peaks. Also, outliers (if present) are not dropped but shown explicitly. Therefore, boxplots are most useful if the distribution is symmetric and they are very useful for detecting outliers.

  • The book doesn't mention violin plots. Are those better or worse than box plots? Why?

    • A violin plot is more informative than a plain box plot. A box plot only shows summary statistics such as mean/median and interquartile ranges. A violin plot shows the full distribution of the data. The usage of a violin plot is especially useful when the data distribution is multimodal (has more than one peak). In those cases, a violin plot clearly shows the presence of different peaks, their position and relative amplitude.



Part 2: DAOST Chapter 3 Questions

  • Looking at Fig 3-1, Janert writes "the data itself shows clearly that the amount of random noise in the data is small". What do you think his argument is?

    • From Figure 3-1, there is a clear relationship between x and y as the data points fall on a relatively smooth curve that gets smaller as x increases. Therefore, like Janert writes, because of this relationship, the random noise in the data is small and the main concern is to determine the nature of the relationship of x and y. (Figure 3-1 from Data Analysis with Open Source Tools by Philipp K. Janert).

  • Can you think of a real-world example of a multivariate relationship like the one in Fig 3-3 (lower right panel)?

    • When testing a drug for medical study, where the amount of drug is on the x-axis and a symptom on the y-axis. First the patients have the same symptoms but then, when taking more the drug, there is a certain group that gets increasing symptoms and other patients get less symptoms.

  • What are the two methods Janert mentions for smoothing noisy data? Can you think of other ones?

    • The two methods for smoothing noisy data are weighted splines and LOESS (locally weighted regression). Those methods approximate the data in a small neighborhood by a polynomial of low order. Another method is moving average, where averages of different subsets of the full dataset are found. Other method is exponential smoothing, which is used for time series data.

  • What are residuals? Why is it a good idea to plot the residuals of your fit?

    • A residual is when the smooth trend of a data is subtracted from the actual data, in other words, it is the error of the smoothed approximation. It is good to plot the residuals to see if there are any systematic trend in the residuals, but if there is a systematic trend, then the fit found is not appropriate for the data. Also, if a residual for a certain data point is very large then it can suggested that the data point is an outlier in the data.

  • Explain in your own words the point of the smooth tube in figure 3-7.

    • The smooth tube is made of the two dashed lines. Those dashed lines are made when the original data points are split into two classes, one class for positive residuals and one for negative residuals (classify if the points fall below or above the solid line (approximation)). The majority of the data points will lie inside the tube (between the two dotted lines), so, with this smoothing tube it can be examined what points fall outside the tube and also if the width of the tube is in correlation with the solid line (more width can tell that the residual value is higher for data points in that area). (Figure 3-7 from Data Analysis with Open Source Tools by Philipp K. Janert).

  • What kind of relationships will a semi-log plot help you discover?

    • A semi-log plot is where only one axis (usually the vertical axis) has been scaled logarithmically (plot of logarithm of y as a function of x). Semi-log plots can help to discover a straight linear relationship between x and logarithm of y. This straight line has slope α(alpha), where α is the scale factor which is useful in many applications. Also the line has offset logC.

  • What kind of functions will loglog plots help you see?

    • Loglog plots can help showing the distribution of data points clearer and are helpful showing if data is following power-law. Loglog plot display power-law relations as a straight line.

  • What the h#ll is banking and what element from our visual system does it use to help us see patterns? What are potential problems with banking?

    • Banking is a graphical method that works on a plot as a whole by changing its aspect ratio. This changing of the aspect ratio of the entire plot is made in such a way so most slopes are at circa 45 degree angle. This is done to get a better feeling if there is a change in the data, but it is hard to see if there is a change in the data if curves are nearly horizontal or vertical.
    Potential problems with banking are that the vertical axis can become very small and then the details of the graph is lost. Also it is becomes more difficult to compare behavior on the far right and far left of the graph. Banking may not be taking to literally and if the aspect ratio is too skewed it can be better to rethink how you visualise the data.

  • I think figure 3-14 makes an important point about linear fits that is rarely made. What is it?

    • Linear fits (linear regression) are not to summarize the data, but instead to predict. In figure 3-14 there is illustrated that when regress x on y is compared to regress y on x, it is not the same solution. So, the data set can be fitted in those both ways and the resulting straight lines are two different lines. Therefore linear fit is not a good tool to give one best fit, it is more of a predictor instead. (Figure 3-14 from Data Analysis with Open Source Tools by Philipp K. Janert).

  • Summarize the discussion of Graphical Analysis and Presentation Graphics on pp. 68-69 in your own words

    • A graphical analysis is when you use graphical methods to investigate data. When doing the analysis new information about the data set can be discovered, and also proper questions to ask will be discovered. In the analysis you can apply different transformations and styles to discover and get deeper understanding of the data.
    A presentation graphics are how you communicate information and results that are already known and understood. There are various things that are recommended, e.g. to make text and labels self explanatory, don’t forget the units, pick a suitable font and font size, and explain error bars.

This is Quantifiably the Best Month to go to the Farmers Market

As part of a homework assignment, I ask my students at Pratt to go out and chart something of interest to them, and Katherine Savarese came back with a simple chart about farmers markets that I loved- it inspired this post.

You probably know that farmers markets are a staple across all five boroughs of New York City, but September happens to be a very special month. Why is that?  Well, it turns out September is the month with the most unique types of fresh produce- forty three to be exact.

To show the current offerings, we charted how many types of fruits and vegetables are available by month, and showed if they were fresh or from storage. 

  • Fresh Fruit
  • Fresh Vegetable
  • Storage Fruit
  • Storage Vegetable

If you miss the month of September, you might be behind the fruit curve, but there are plenty more months of vegetables left.  The chart shows that fresh vegetables are available 9 out of 12 months, but fruit is only available 5 out of 12. Now if only we could quantify how delicious the produce is…
